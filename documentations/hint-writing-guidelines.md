# Hint-Writing Guidelines for Gap-Fill Vocabulary Questions

## Purpose

A hint helps the student **understand the question well enough to reason toward the answer themselves**. It is comprehension scaffolding — never an answer key.

The test: *If a student could pick the correct option from the hint alone, without understanding the sentence, the hint has failed.*

A hint does two jobs at once:
1. It removes the vocabulary barriers that stop a weaker reader from parsing the sentence.
2. It clarifies the situation the sentence describes, so the student understands the frame they're reasoning within.

Neither job involves revealing what goes in the blank.

---

## Core Rules

### 1. Gloss the keywords that carry the meaning — not just one word

Identify the **2–3 words or phrases** that a weaker student genuinely needs in order to understand the sentence. Translate each one.

- Treat compound nouns and fixed phrases as single units (e.g. gloss a two-word term as one item, not two).
- Choose the words that actually carry the sentence's meaning — the ones that, if misunderstood, would leave the student unable to reason about the gap.
- One glossed word is rarely enough. A single translation seldom unlocks a sentence a student is stuck on.

### 2. Add a brief "what's happening here" gloss

Include **one short clause** explaining the *situation or relationship* the sentence describes — the frame, not the fill.

This orients the student: what is going on, who is doing what, or what relationship the sentence sets up. It is the difference between handing someone isolated words and helping them see the whole picture.

### 3. Never reveal the answer — in any form

The hint must not:
- Contain the target word.
- Contain the target word's translation (not even as a substring of a longer word or phrase).
- Paraphrase or define the answer's meaning closely enough to give it away.

Describe the *situation* the answer relates to, never the answer's meaning. A frame-level description ("the sentence describes something that signals a problem") is acceptable; a near-definition of the missing word is not.

### 4. Every glossed word must actually be in the sentence

- Each keyword you gloss must physically appear in the question text.
- No glossed keyword may be one of the answer options (correct or distractor).

### 5. Don't gloss trivially easy words

If a word is obvious to the target proficiency level, glossing it isn't scaffolding — it's noise. Choose words a genuine learner at the intended level might actually stumble on. Skip the ones they'll already know.

### 6. Handling deliberately simple sentences

Some sentences have no cluster of difficult vocabulary. When there is genuinely little hard lexis to gloss:

- Gloss the one meaningful word that exists, **and** lean more on the situational explanation to carry the hint.
- A shorter hint is acceptable here — don't invent difficulty that isn't there or pad with trivial glosses.
- The situational "what's happening here" clause still applies and does most of the work.

---

## Format

```
keyword1 = translation، keyword2 = translation — [short clause explaining the situation, without revealing the answer]
```

- List glossed keywords first, then a dash, then the situational explanation.
- Keep it concise: enough to unlock comprehension, not a paragraph.
- Maintain consistent punctuation and structure across all items in a set.

---

## Self-Check Before Finalizing

Run every hint against this checklist:

- [ ] Does it gloss **2–3 meaningful keywords** (fewer only if the sentence is genuinely simple)?
- [ ] Is each glossed keyword **physically present** in the sentence?
- [ ] Is each glossed keyword **not** one of the answer options?
- [ ] Does it include a **situational explanation** of what the sentence is doing?
- [ ] Is the **target word absent** from the hint?
- [ ] Is the **target's translation absent** from the hint (including as a substring)?
- [ ] Does the explanation **avoid defining or paraphrasing** the answer?
- [ ] Are the glossed words **appropriately difficult** for the level (not trivial)?

If any box is unchecked, the hint needs revision.

---

## The Division of Labor

Keep clear which field does what:

| Field | Job | Reveals the answer? |
|---|---|---|
| **Hint** | Helps the student understand the question and reason toward the answer | **No** |
| **Feedback (correct)** | Confirms and explains why the answer fits, shown *after* the student responds | Yes |
| **Feedback (incorrect)** | Names the answer, explains it, and contrasts the distractors, shown *after* the student responds | Yes |

The answer belongs in the feedback, never in the hint. The feedback fields are free to reveal and explain the answer precisely because the student only sees them once they've already committed to a choice.
